> *It's not DNS.*<br/>
> *There's no way it's DNS.*<br/>
> *It was DNS.*
>
> — the ancient SRE haiku, recited at every incident retro since the dawn of anycast

Computer science famously has two hard problems: cache invalidation, naming things, and off-by-one errors. DNS is what happens when you take *naming things*, distribute it across every machine on Earth, and then bolt *cache invalidation* onto it with a field literally called TTL. It is all of the hard problems at once, running in production, since 1983.

So naturally I maintain a Ruby gem for it.

## A Release Cadence Best Described as "Geological"

[dnsmadeeasy](https://github.com/kigster/dnsmadeeasy) is my Ruby client for the [DNS Made Easy](https://www.dnsmadeeasy.com/) API. I published `0.1.0` in December 2017, it quietly accumulated [fifty-odd thousand downloads](https://rubygems.org/gems/dnsmadeeasy), and then — after `0.4.0` in April 2020 — nothing. For six years. In my defense, a lot happened between those releases: a pandemic, several jobs, one baby. Software erosion is real, but apparently my DNS records outlasted three employers, which tells you something about DNS records and possibly about me.

Today the gem wakes up as **1.0.1**, and it's a full rewrite. The headline feature: **real RFC zone-file support**, wrapped in the workflow you already know from Terraform:

```bash
dmez zone export kig.re --output=kig.re.zone   # current state → file
$EDITOR kig.re.zone                            # edit the desired state
dmez zone plan  kig.re.zone                    # diff: what would change?
dmez zone apply kig.re.zone --yes              # make it so
```

No HCL. No state file to lose, lock, or tearfully restore from S3 versioning. The zone file *is* the desired state; the DNS Made Easy API *is* the current state; `plan` is the diff between them. Terraform's best idea, minus the seventeen others.

There's also `dmez zone validate` and `dmez zone format` (yes, a formatter for zone files — `rubocop -A` for your DNS, alignment included), and exports come in `rfc`, `json`, or `yaml` flavors. One deliberate design decision I'm disproportionately proud of: **stdout carries only payload** — zone files, plan output — while every status box, warning, and progress message goes to stderr. You can pipe `dmez` into anything without your zone file acquiring decorative Unicode borders.

## ANAME: the Cryptid of Record Types

Here is a fact that has ruined at least one afternoon for every person reading this: **you cannot put a CNAME at the apex of a zone.** [RFC 1034](https://datatracker.ietf.org/doc/html/rfc1034) says a CNAME must be the *only* record at its name, and your apex is already busy holding the SOA and NS records. So `kig.re` cannot be a CNAME to my CDN, even though that is *obviously* what I want.

Every DNS provider invented its own workaround, and DNS Made Easy's is the **ANAME** — a record that walks like a CNAME, quacks like a CNAME, but resolves to A records at the edge, so the RFC police have nothing to pin on you at query time. It's a lovely feature with one catch: `ANAME` doesn't exist in any RFC, which means it doesn't exist in the standard zone-file grammar, which means most tooling looks at it the way OmniGraffle looks at a `foreignObject` — politely, and then not at all.

The gem treats ANAME as a first-class citizen. By default, `dmez zone export` writes the ANAME line as-is — non-standard, but it round-trips perfectly through `plan` and `apply`, which is what you want when DME is both the source and the destination:

```text
$ORIGIN kig.re.
$TTL 3600
@        IN ANAME   t.sni.global.fastly.net.
www      IN CNAME   t.sni.global.fastly.net.
@        IN MX      10 mail.protonmail.ch.
@        IN MX      20 mailsec.protonmail.ch.
@        IN TXT     "v=spf1 include:_spf.protonmail.ch ~all"
```

*(Lightly trimmed. The real file has more `TXT` verification records than a conspiracy theorist has browser bookmarks.)*

But when you need a *portable*, RFC-compliant zone — migrating providers, feeding a validator, archiving — pass `--strict-rfc`, and ANAMEs get flattened into their currently-resolved A records, which is exactly what DNS Made Easy's own web-UI export does:

```bash
$ dmez zone export kig.re --strict-rfc --output=kig.re.zone
Flattened ANAME @ -> t.sni.global.fastly.net. into A 146.75.31.52,
  151.101.3.52, 151.101.67.52, 151.101.131.52, 151.101.195.52
  (point-in-time snapshot)
```

That warning goes to stderr, naturally. And if an ANAME target doesn't resolve, the export *fails loudly* instead of silently dropping your apex — because a zone file that's quietly missing its apex is the kind of gift that keeps on giving, at 3am, to whoever is on call.

> [!WARNING]
> Flattening is a *point-in-time snapshot* — the gem tells you so in the warning. If Fastly renumbers next Tuesday, your flattened A records are stale. That's the entire reason ANAME exists. Flatten for portability and compliance; keep the ANAME for daily life.

Don't take my word for the flattening — ask the horse directly:

```bash
$ dig +short A kig.re
146.75.31.52
151.101.3.52
151.101.67.52
151.101.131.52
151.101.195.52
```

Five A records at the apex, all Fastly, none of them hand-typed by me. And yes, I'm publishing my DNS records on my blog. DNS is the world's most public database — the only people who don't know your records are the ones who haven't run `dig` yet.

## Where DNS Made Easy Fits (or: the County Clerk and the Switchboard)

People conflate two services because both come stapled to the word "domain": the **registrar**, where you *buy* the name, and the **DNS host**, which *answers questions* about it. I keep them separate on purpose. I register domains at [Namecheap](https://www.namecheap.com) or [Gandi](https://www.gandi.net) — whoever has the better price for that TLD that week — and I host the DNS for all of them at DNS Made Easy.

![How DNS resolution actually flows: stub resolver → resolving name server → root → TLD → authoritative](/assets/images/posts/dns/how-dns-works.png)

Follow the arrows: your laptop asks a resolving name server, which asks a root server ("who runs `.re`?"), which points at the TLD server, which points at the **authoritative name server** — the only box in that diagram that actually knows my records. The registrar appears nowhere in the hot path. Its one DNS-related job is telling the TLD which authoritative servers to name, and that's the NS delegation. So the first thing I do after buying a domain — before the confirmation email finishes landing — is walk into the registrar's panel and change the nameservers:

```bash
$ dig +short NS kig.re
ns0.dnsmadeeasy.com.
ns1.dnsmadeeasy.com.
ns2.dnsmadeeasy.com.
ns3.dnsmadeeasy.com.
ns4.dnsmadeeasy.com.
```

Registrar for the paperwork, DME for the answers. The county clerk records the deed; you don't ask them to answer your doorbell.

(And yes, my blog lives on `.re` — the TLD of Réunion, a French island in the Indian Ocean. The resolution path could not care less, which is rather the point.)

## The Numbers, Because You Should Ask for Numbers

Why have I kept all my domains on one mid-sized provider for a decade, through an acquisition, while the entire internet consolidated onto two CDNs? Because the answers come back *fast* and the service *does not go down*. Receipts:

**Measured from my desk, just now.** Nine authoritative queries against `ns0`–`ns2.dnsmadeeasy.com` for `kig.re`, from the SF Bay Area:

```bash
$ for ns in ns0 ns1 ns2; do
    dig @${ns}.dnsmadeeasy.com kig.re A | grep 'Query time'
  done
;; Query time: 11 msec
;; Query time: 7 msec
;; Query time: 6 msec    # rounds 2–3: 8–10 msec, every time
```

Six to eleven milliseconds, authoritative, no resolver cache involved. That's anycast doing its job — I'm one hop from a PoP, and so, statistically, are you. For scale: a [December 2025 comparison](https://hokstadconsulting.com/blog/dns-providers-resolution-speed) clocked the fastest names in the business — Cloudflare at 10.36 ms, G-Core at 21.17 ms, Google Public DNS at 19.16 ms (resolver-side numbers, but it calibrates the ballpark). My boutique DNS host is answering *authoritatively* in the same single digits. [DNSPerf](https://www.dnsperf.com/dns-provider/dnsmadeeasy-by-digicert) tests them every minute from 200+ locations if you want the live worldwide picture — they're listed as "DNSMadeEasy by DigiCert" now.

**Uptime.** Their [homepage today](https://www.dnsmadeeasy.com/) claims **"22+ years of 100% uptime — the longest in the industry."** DigiCert's [2022 acquisition press release](https://www.prnewswire.com/news-releases/digicert-acquires-dns-made-easy-extending-its-leadership-in-digital-trust-with-enterprise-grade-managed-dns-services-301564617.html), presumably drafted by people billing by the hour, went with the more lawyerly "proven record of five 9's availability for more than a decade." ## The Actual Reason I Did All This: Email

Here's the confession at the bottom of the changelog. I didn't rewrite this gem out of some noble love for RFC 1035. I rewrote it because **every transactional-email provider onboarding is a small DNS hazing ritual**, and I have a lot of domains.

Sign up a domain with [Resend](https://resend.com) or [SendGrid](https://sendgrid.com) and you're handed a pile of records: a DKIM key, an SPF `TXT` on a `send` subdomain, an MX for the bounce path, a DMARC policy — four to seven records, each lovingly hand-pasted into a web UI that signs you out mid-task to ask about cookies. Now multiply by the family portfolio: my wife's tax practice, the umbrella site, the game, this blog. I have typed `v=spf1` into more text fields than I have typed my own name.

Now it's a block appended to a zone file:

```text
; --- resend.com onboarding, the whole ritual ---
send              300 IN MX      10 feedback-smtp.us-east-1.amazonses.com.
send              300 IN TXT     "v=spf1 include:amazonses.com ~all"
resend._domainkey 300 IN TXT     "p=MIGfMA0GCSqGSIb3DQEB...IDAQAB"  ; truncated, obviously
_dmarc            300 IN TXT     "v=DMARC1; p=none;"
```

Then the Terraform ceremony, minus the sacrifice:

```bash
$ dmez zone plan agentica.group.zone
Create
  - send.agentica.group MX 10 feedback-smtp.us-east-1.amazonses.com. (ttl=300)
  - send.agentica.group TXT "v=spf1 include:amazonses.com ~all" (ttl=300)
  - resend._domainkey.agentica.group TXT "p=MIGf..." (ttl=300)
  - _dmarc.agentica.group TXT "v=DMARC1; p=none;" (ttl=300)

$ dmez zone apply agentica.group.zone --add-only --yes
```

*(Output abridged; DKIM key fictional, before you reach for `base64 -d`.)*

Note `--add-only` — the blast-radius flag. `apply` has three modes: the default `--merge` (creates + updates), `--add-only` (only create what's missing — perfect for onboarding, *incapable* of deleting your MX records), and `--delete-only` for the confident. Adding email records to a live business domain with a tool that structurally cannot touch existing records is the closest DNS gets to a good night's sleep.

One zone block, one plan, one apply, per domain. Resend goes green on all its checks a few minutes later. *One fell swoop.*

## There's a Ruby API Under There Too

The CLI is a veneer over a proper client, so everything above is scriptable:

```ruby
require 'dnsmadeeasy'

DME.domains                                # all your zones
DME.records_for('kig.re')                  # every record, as objects
DME.create_aname_record('kig.re', '',      # apex ANAME, programmatically
                        't.sni.global.fastly.net')
```

Credentials live in a small YAML file in your home directory (multiple accounts supported — see the [README](https://github.com/kigster/dnsmadeeasy#readme)), and the new `dmez` binary sits alongside the classic `dme` for the API-level commands.

## Coda

```bash
gem install dnsmadeeasy
dmez zone export yourdomain.com
```

The code is on [GitHub](https://github.com/kigster/dnsmadeeasy), the gem is on [RubyGems](https://rubygems.org/gems/dnsmadeeasy), and issues/PRs are welcome — the rewrite ships with an actual test suite, so your contribution won't be load-bearing folklore.

And remember the deepest truth our industry has produced: there is no such thing as DNS propagation. There are only caches, patiently lying to you until their TTL runs out. Set your TTLs like you set your expectations: low — and `plan` before you `apply`.
